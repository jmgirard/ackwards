# xia2019 — why the naive/ordinal fit indices under WLSMV are treated as optimistic

**Full citation.** Xia, Y., & Yang, Y. (2019). RMSEA, CFI, and TLI in structural
equation modeling with ordered categorical data: The story they tell depends on
the estimation methods. *Behavior Research Methods, 51*(1), 409–428.
https://doi.org/10.3758/s13428-018-1055-2 *(DOI printed on source p. 409;
published online 4 June 2018)*.

Extraction: verified 2026-07-19 (M69) — citation and the core finding

## Why this is a primary source

The source cited when the ESEM engine and `tidy()` warn that RMSEA/CFI/TLI under
the categorical (WLSMV/ULSMV/DWLS/ULS) estimators are not comparable to the
conventional ML-derived cutoffs ([[hu1999]]) — they read too optimistically.

## Extracted values

Abstract finding, verbatim (p. 409): "The results showed that DWLS and ULS lead
to smaller RMSEA and larger CFI and TLI values than does ML for all manipulated
conditions, regardless of whether or not the indices are scaled. Applying the
conventional cutoffs to DWLS and ULS, therefore, has a pronounced tendency not
to discover model–data misfit."

- Supports the package's direction-of-bias claim (`R/engine_esem.R:269`,
  `R/tidy.R:44`): the categorical-estimator RMSEA/CFI/TLI are **optimistic**
  (smaller RMSEA, larger CFI/TLI), so ML cutoffs over-accept.

**One nuance worth carrying (standing fact).** Xia & Yang's optimism is
**DWLS/ULS-vs-ML, and holds "regardless of whether or not the indices are
scaled."** The code frames the issue as *naive-vs-scaled* ("the naive
CFI/TLI/RMSEA are badly optimistic … only the scaled test/indices are defensible
here"). Reporting the scaled indices is a defensible lavaan choice, but it is not
one Xia & Yang endorse as *sufficient*: they find even the scaled DWLS/ULS
indices optimistic against ML cutoffs. So the citation backs the *direction* of
the caveat, not the "scaled ⇒ defensible" inference layered on top of it.

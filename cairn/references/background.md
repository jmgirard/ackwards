## kotov2017 — Kotov et al. (2017) _(HiTOP founding paper)_

Kotov, R., Krueger, R. F., Watson, D., Achenbach, T. M., Althoff, R. R.,
Bagby, R. M., … Zimmerman, M. (2017). The Hierarchical Taxonomy of
Psychopathology (HiTOP): A dimensional alternative to traditional
nosologies. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology, 126*(4), 454–477.
https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000258

The 40-author consortium paper defining the HiTOP framework (author count
verified exactly). Its own top-down definitions: **superspectra** are
"extremely broad dimensions comprised of multiple spectra, such as a general
factor of psychopathology"; **spectra** "larger constellations of syndromes";
**subfactors** "groups of closely related syndromes"; **syndromes**
"composites of related components/traits"; then components/traits. ("Super
spectrum" was our singularization — the paper's term is *superspectra*.)
Every
application in `applications.md` targets or extends this structure, and
Forbes's HiTOP affiliation is why [[forbes2023]] exists. Cite for Intro
framing; the empirical hierarchy-building method HiTOP studies actually use
at each level is bass-ackwards.

## schmid1957 — Schmid & Leiman (1957) _(the bottom-up contrast)_

Schmid, J., & Leiman, J. M. (1957). The development of hierarchical factor
solutions. *Psychometrika, 22*(1), 53–61. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02289209

The Schmid–Leiman transformation: an oblique higher-order solution is
re-expressed as a single orthogonal layer in which each variable loads on a
general factor plus group factors. The canonical **bottom-up**
hierarchicalization — the "received wisdom" [[goldberg2006]] §1 positions
bass-ackwards against. Verified verbatim at p. 348: "the use of such
orthogonalized hierarchical solutions has now become the received wisdom in
analyses of cognitive abilities (e.g., Carroll, 1993). But, what exactly can
one make of factors that are independent of all factors at other levels?" —
and Goldberg cites Schmid and Leiman there as "(1957; see also Yung et al.,
1999)", the same pairing this page makes. SL/higher-order SEM is
explicitly out of scope for ackwards (DESIGN §2; CLAUDE.md); this is the
citation for that boundary.

## yung1999 — Yung, Thissen, & McLeod (1999) _(the formal relationship)_

Yung, Y.-F., Thissen, D., & McLeod, L. D. (1999). On the relationship
between the higher-order factor model and the hierarchical factor model.
*Psychometrika, 64*(2), 113–128. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02294531

Proves SL is a *constrained* hierarchical factor model, and any unconstrained
hierarchical model is equivalent to a higher-order model **with direct
effects** (loadings from the higher-order factors onto the manifest
variables); the two classes are not equivalent in general. The precise
relation (abstract, p. 113): the class of higher-order factor models "(without
direct effects of higher-order factors) is **nested within** the class of
unconstrained hierarchical factor models" — and the bi-factor
(general-plus-specific) model is a special case of the hierarchical factor
model (p. 114). This is the
technical sense of "hierarchical" against which Goldberg concedes
bass-ackwards is "sequential" rather than truly hierarchical
([[goldberg2006]] §5) — the honest-caveat citation for the manuscript's
Discussion, paired with the part-whole-correlation caveat.

## saucier1996 — Saucier & Goldberg (1996) _(Big Five robustness; ruled out as comparability source)_

Saucier, G., & Goldberg, L. R. (1996). Evidence for the Big Five in analyses
of familiar English personality adjectives. *European Journal of
Personality, 10*(1), 61–77.
https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1099-0984(199603)10:1%3C61::AID-EJP246%3E3.0.CO;2-D

435 familiar adjectives, N = 899 pooled (507 self + 392 peer ratings): PCA,
first five components varimax-rotated; Big Five robust under judge-driven
variable selection (anti-"prestructuring" evidence — the paper's stated test
is "the hypothesis that finding the Big Five depends on biasing the variable
selection with an investigator's preferred non-familiar terms").

They examined solutions with **five to 12 factors**. The average correlations
of the first five factors with their counterparts in the six- through
11-factor solutions were **.95, .90, .93, .91, .89, .90**, and **.84** for the
12-factor solution — so the earlier "(.95–.84)" shorthand named the endpoints
of a series that is not monotone (its 6–11 minimum is .89). Marker-set factor
score correlations run **.76–.98** across the two Big-Five marker sets (the
.76 is the fifth/Intellect factor against the unipolar markers; Table 1).

**Checked 2026-07-16 as a candidate source for the comparability benchmarks:
negative** — no split-halves, no comparability coefficients (re-confirmed
2026-07-19: "split" and "comparab" occur zero times in the source). ⚠ It
*does* report **congruence correlations** of .93, .84, .93, .85, .86 between
the two marker sets — those are congruence coefficients between marker sets,
**not** Everett/Goldberg-style comparability coefficients, and must not be
borrowed as such (see [[goldberg1990]] ⚠ block; the real sources are
[[saucier1997]] + [[saucier2005]]). Keep for Big Five robustness/framing.

## widiger2019 — Widiger et al. (2019) _(HiTOP × AMPD review)_

Widiger, T. A., Bach, B., Chmielewski, M., Clark, L. A., DeYoung, C.,
Hopwood, C. J., … Thomas, K. M. (2019). Criterion A of the AMPD in HiTOP.
*Journal of Personality Assessment, 101*(4), 345–355.
https://doi.org/10.1080/00223891.2018.1465431

Consortium review of whether DSM-5 AMPD Criterion A (self/interpersonal
deficits) belongs in HiTOP, largely via the g-PD/p-factor literature
(confirmed: "g-PD" appears throughout, "p-factor" alongside it). Its stated
purpose is "to discuss the potential inclusion and placement of the
self-interpersonal deficits of the DSM-5 Section III Criterion A within
HiTOP" (abstract, p. 345). No hierarchical structural analysis of its own —
context only; omit from method citations. It does, at p. 347, summarize one
bass-ackwards application not otherwise on our shelf: Muñoz-Champel,
Gutiérrez, Peri, & Torrubia used "Goldberg's exploratory bass-ackwards method"
on self-reported PD symptom criteria. *(Moved here from applications.md
2026-07-16.)*

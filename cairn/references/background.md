## kotov2017 — Kotov et al. (2017) _(HiTOP founding paper)_

Kotov, R., Krueger, R. F., Watson, D., Achenbach, T. M., Althoff, R. R.,
Bagby, R. M., … Zimmerman, M. (2017). The Hierarchical Taxonomy of
Psychopathology (HiTOP): A dimensional alternative to traditional
nosologies. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology, 126*(4), 454–477.
https://doi.org/10.1037/abn0000258

The 40-author consortium paper defining the HiTOP framework (super
spectrum → spectra → subfactors → syndromes → components/traits). Every
application in `applications.md` targets or extends this structure, and
Forbes's HiTOP affiliation is why [[forbes2023]] exists. Cite for Intro
framing; the empirical hierarchy-building method HiTOP studies actually use
at each level is bass-ackwards.

## schmid1957 — Schmid & Leiman (1957) _(the bottom-up contrast)_

Schmid, J., & Leiman, J. M. (1957). The development of hierarchical factor
solutions. *Psychometrika, 22*(1), 53–61. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02289209

The Schmid–Leiman transformation: an oblique higher-order solution is
re-expressed as a single orthogonal layer in which each variable loads on a
general factor plus group factors. The canonical **bottom-up**
hierarchicalization — the "received wisdom" [[goldberg2006]] §1 positions
bass-ackwards against ("what exactly can one make of factors that are
independent of all factors at other levels?"). SL/higher-order SEM is
explicitly out of scope for ackwards (DESIGN §2; CLAUDE.md); this is the
citation for that boundary.

## yung1999 — Yung, Thissen, & McLeod (1999) _(the formal relationship)_

Yung, Y.-F., Thissen, D., & McLeod, L. D. (1999). On the relationship
between the higher-order factor model and the hierarchical factor model.
*Psychometrika, 64*(2), 113–128. https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02294531

Proves SL is a *constrained* hierarchical factor model, and any unconstrained
hierarchical model is equivalent to a higher-order model **with direct
effects**; the two classes are not equivalent in general. This is the
technical sense of "hierarchical" against which Goldberg concedes
bass-ackwards is "sequential" rather than truly hierarchical
([[goldberg2006]] §5) — the honest-caveat citation for the manuscript's
Discussion, paired with the part-whole-correlation caveat.

## saucier1996 — Saucier & Goldberg (1996) _(Big Five robustness; ruled out as comparability source)_

Saucier, G., & Goldberg, L. R. (1996). Evidence for the Big Five in analyses
of familiar English personality adjectives. *European Journal of
Personality, 10*(1), 61–77.
https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1099-0984(199603)10:1%3C61::AID-EJP246%3E3.0.CO;2-D

435 familiar adjectives, N = 899 pooled self + peer: PCA + varimax; Big Five
robust under judge-driven variable selection (anti-"prestructuring"
evidence); 5→12-factor invariance via average factor-score correlations
(.95–.84); marker-set score correlations .76–.98. **Checked 2026-07-16 as a
candidate source for the comparability benchmarks: negative** — no
split-halves, no comparability coefficients (see [[goldberg1990]] ⚠ block;
the real sources are [[saucier1997]] + [[saucier2005]]). Keep for Big Five
robustness/framing.

## widiger2019 — Widiger et al. (2019) _(HiTOP × AMPD review)_

Widiger, T. A., Bach, B., Chmielewski, M., Clark, L. A., DeYoung, C.,
Hopwood, C. J., … Thomas, K. M. (2019). Criterion A of the AMPD in HiTOP.
*Journal of Personality Assessment, 101*(4), 345–355.
https://doi.org/10.1080/00223891.2018.1465431

Consortium review of whether DSM-5 AMPD Criterion A (self/interpersonal
deficits) belongs in HiTOP, largely via the g-PD/p-factor literature. No
hierarchical structural analysis of its own — context only; omit from method
citations. *(Moved here from applications.md 2026-07-16.)*
